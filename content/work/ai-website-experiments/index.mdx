## What it is

An ongoing series of small, real websites built with AI
assistance and deployed end-to-end. The point isn&rsquo;t to
prove that AI can write code &mdash; that&rsquo;s settled.
The point is to figure out what the day-to-day workflow
actually looks like when you take it seriously as a
production toolchain.

So far this has meant: working with Claude to plan,
generate, and debug code; pushing to GitHub; deploying on
Vercel; and watching what breaks.

## Why it exists

I started doing this because I wanted to ship something. The
gap between &ldquo;I have an idea&rdquo; and &ldquo;there is
a live website&rdquo; used to take me weeks. With the right
workflow it now takes hours.

That&rsquo;s a meaningful change, and I don&rsquo;t think the
implications are well-understood yet. Most of the public
conversation is split between people who oversell it and
people who dismiss it. The interesting work is in between:
*what does it actually do well, what does it do badly, and
what does the production workflow look like when you trust
it for real?*

This project page is the index for those notes.

## How it works

The workflow I&rsquo;ve settled on (for now):

1. **Write the brief first, in plain English.** A document
   that says what the site is for, who it&rsquo;s for, what
   it must do, what it must not do, and what it should feel
   like. This is the most important step and the easiest
   one to skip.
2. **Plan the architecture before writing code.** What pages?
   What content types? What stack? Decide once. Decide on
   paper.
3. **Generate in small pieces, not all at once.** Page by
   page, component by component. Easier to review, easier
   to fix, easier to learn from.
4. **Deploy early and often.** Push to GitHub the first day.
   Get a live URL the first day. Iterate against the real
   site, not against an idea of a site.
5. **Read everything that gets generated.** This is
   non-negotiable. Code you don&rsquo;t understand is code
   you can&rsquo;t maintain.
